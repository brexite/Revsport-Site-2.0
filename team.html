About the team...

500,000BC, Africa

The first humans appeared in the African continent. As the sun set each day on the plains, these early humans chased their food with spears and primitive axes, but was always outrun by the more prized creatures. Antelopes and gazelles were on another level of pace, and this got our prehistoric forefathers thinking the most important question ever concieved.

What if fast?

1000AD, Polynesia

The Polynesians are now well known as having a reputation as great navigators - navigating their canoes across the Pacific Ocean to places as far flung as Hawaii and Fiji. However, upon discovering the Easter Island, they wanted to venture further across the ocean. This plan was dashed by the complete lack of ability to manouvre their canoes for further distances over the crashing waves of the Pacific. Upon returning to Polynesia, they got to work creating a system to allow them to absorb the waves under their canoes.

200 years later, they managed to sail their ships all the way to the isles of what is now modern-day New Zealand. The technology that allowed them to reach so far?

The Polynesians had discovered Double-Wishbone Suspension

1065 AD, England

The Bronze Age is coming to an end, and the Battle of Hastings has been raging on for three decades prior. English blacksmiths are trying to develop a new kind of horse, one made from the strongest metal they have access to. Their final design was a completely alien design in Pre-Norman Britain, but with the gift of hindsight, Nissan took those centries old designs and directly converted their drawings into their Modern-Day GT3 car.

The age of speed could have started all those years ago, but the invasion from the Normans just a year later proved to be fatal. It turns out William the Conqueror was secretly born in Switzerland, and their festering hate of cars had seeped into William's blood and resulted in the FIA GT3 Regulations to be pushed back another 1000 years.
